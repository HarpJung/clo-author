## Suggested Contribution Statement

### Draft 1 (for a corporate governance / accountability venue)

"Despite decades of research on boards of directors, the governance literature has studied only one tier of the multi-tiered governance architecture that characterizes most private firms. We provide the first systematic analysis of how the three tiers of board governance -- directors, observers, and advisors -- function as an integrated system. Drawing on interviews with practitioners who operate across all three tiers, we document that the three-tier structure creates a novel accountability configuration: observers possess full informational access to board deliberations but bear no fiduciary obligations, while advisors exercise influence through social capital without formal governance standing. This configuration produces a previously undocumented principal class -- 'high-information, low-duty' actors -- that falls outside the standard agency theory framework. Our findings generate testable propositions about how liability exposure drives role selection, how board dynamics degrade as non-director participation grows, and how the same governance labels mask different realities across ownership structures."

### Draft 2 (for a management / strategy venue)

"We study a ubiquitous but academically invisible governance phenomenon: the simultaneous operation of board directors, board observers, and board advisors in private firms. Board observers attend an estimated 82% of VC-backed company board meetings, and advisory boards exist in approximately 38% of private companies, yet no prior research examines how these three roles interact. Using semi-structured interviews with practitioners positioned at different nodes of the governance architecture, we develop a framework for understanding the three-tier system as an institutional response to the monitoring-advising tradeoff (Adams and Ferreira, 2007). We identify five themes: (1) role definitions diverge sharply from practice; (2) liability exposure is a primary mechanism driving role selection; (3) information access and accountability are inversely related across tiers; (4) governance architecture evolves over the firm lifecycle; and (5) identical role labels mask different governance realities across ownership structures."

## Suggested Framing Strategy

### Opening Hook

Open with the striking contrast: board observers are present in 82% of VC-backed deals, advisory boards in 38% of private companies, and yet the academic literature contains zero empirical studies of either role. The entire governance literature studies only one of the three tiers.

### Literature Positioning

Position the paper at the intersection of three literature streams:

1. **Board composition and effectiveness** (Adams et al. 2010) -- extend from directors to the full governance architecture
2. **VC contracting and governance** (Kaplan & Stromberg 2003; Ewens & Malenko 2024) -- open the black box of what observer rights mean
3. **Monitoring-advising tradeoff** (Adams & Ferreira 2007) -- show the three-tier system as practice's solution to the theoretical tradeoff

### Theoretical Contribution

Frame the "high-information, low-duty" principal class as a genuine extension of agency theory. In Jensen and Meckling's (1976) framework, principals delegate authority to agents and face agency costs from information asymmetry. Observers reverse the typical problem: they have full information (no asymmetry with the agent) but no authority and no duty to act. This creates a novel agency problem not addressed in existing theory.

### Methodological Justification

Cite Eisenhardt (1989) and Gioia et al. (2013) for the appropriateness of interview-based, theory-building research when studying novel phenomena. Note that observer and advisor dynamics are tacit knowledge that cannot be extracted from public filings or databases -- qualitative methods are not just appropriate but necessary.
