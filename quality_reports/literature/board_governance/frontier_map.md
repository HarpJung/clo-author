# Frontier Map: Three-Tier Board Governance Architecture

**Project:** Board Governance -- Directors, Observers, and Advisors
**Researcher:** Harp Jung, Harvard
**Date:** 2026-03-20

---

## What Has Been Done

### Tier 1: Board of Directors (Extensively Studied)

The formal board of directors is among the most studied institutions in all of economics and management.

**Board Composition and Structure:**
- Optimal board size and composition as a function of firm characteristics (Coles et al. 2008; Raheja 2005)
- Endogeneity of board structure -- boards are chosen, not random (Hermalin & Weisbach 1998, 2003)
- Independence, insider/outsider ratios, and their (weak) relationship to firm performance (Adams et al. 2010 survey)

**Monitoring Function:**
- Boards as monitors of management on behalf of shareholders (Fama & Jensen 1983)
- Board monitoring intensity and its costs (Faleye et al. 2011)
- CEO turnover as the key observable monitoring outcome (Hermalin & Weisbach 1998)

**Advising Function:**
- Boards as providers of strategic advice and resources (Hillman & Dalziel 2003)
- The monitoring-advising tradeoff: too much monitoring chills information sharing (Adams & Ferreira 2007)
- Director capital (human and social) as determinant of advisory quality (Reeb & Zhao 2013)

**Board Dynamics:**
- What boards actually do in meetings (Schwartz-Ziv & Weisbach 2013)
- Communication and decision-making within boards (Malenko 2014)
- Busy directors and capacity constraints (Fich & Shivdasani 2006; Field et al. 2013)

**D&O Insurance:**
- D&O premiums as governance quality signals (Core 2000)
- D&O moral hazard -- insurance reduces monitoring effort (Lin et al. 2011)
- D&O and acquisition outcomes, loan spreads (Lin et al. 2011, 2013)

### Tier 2: VC/PE Board Governance (Well-Studied for Director Seats)

**VC Contracting:**
- Comprehensive inventory of VC contract terms including board seats, voting rights, and (briefly) observer rights (Kaplan & Stromberg 2003, 2004)
- Control rights allocation contingent on performance (Kaplan & Stromberg 2003)
- Contract specialization and standardization across VC firms (Bengtsson & Bernhardt 2014)

**VC Monitoring:**
- VCs increase board representation during periods requiring active oversight (Lerner 1995)
- VC proximity affects monitoring intensity and outcomes (Bernstein et al. 2016)
- VC involvement accelerates professionalization (Hellmann & Puri 2002)

**Startup Board Dynamics:**
- Board composition evolves over startup lifecycle (Ewens & Malenko 2024)
- Independent directors in startups (Broughman 2010, 2013)

### Cross-Cutting Themes (Partially Addressed)

**Interlocking Directorates:**
- Clayton Act Section 8 prohibitions
- Economic effects of board interlocks (Hallock 1997)
- Common ownership and competition (Azar et al. 2018)

**Family Firm Governance:**
- Family ownership, control, and management effects on value (Villalonga & Amit 2006)
- Advisory boards as governance mechanism in family firms (scattered references)

**Comparative Corporate Governance:**
- One-tier vs. two-tier board systems across countries (Aguilera & Jackson 2010)
- German Aufsichtsrat/Vorstand/Beirat system as a formal multi-tier model

## What Has NOT Been Done

### Critical Gaps (Remaining After Packin & Alon-Beck 2025)

**Recent development:** Packin & Alon-Beck (2025), "Board Observers," *University of Illinois Law Review*, Vol. 2025, No. 5, is the first academic article specifically about board observers. It covers legal status, regulatory landscape (antitrust, CFIUS, ERISA), and uses NVCA survey data. Amornsiripanitch, Gompers & Xuan (JLEO, 2019) report observer industry experience rates as a secondary finding.

**However, the following gaps remain open:**

1. **Board observers in business journals.** No accounting, finance, or strategy journal paper studies observers. Packin & Alon-Beck is a law review article with legal/regulatory focus. The practitioner experience of how observers function in governance -- and the accountability consequences -- is undocumented in business scholarship.

2. **The three-tier system as an integrated architecture.** No paper -- including Packin & Alon-Beck -- studies how directors, observers, and advisors interact as a governance system. The literature treats each role in isolation.

3. **Liability as a role-selection mechanism.** No paper studies why individuals choose (or firms assign) observer status vs. director status. Toj's interview reveals that liability exposure is a key driver: "we don't want the responsibility." This mechanism is undocumented in the literature.

4. **The "high-information, low-duty" principal class.** No theoretical or empirical treatment of actors who have full information access to board deliberations but bear no fiduciary duty. This is a novel concept in agency theory.

5. **D&O insurance in private firms.** The D&O literature studies public firms almost exclusively. How D&O affects governance in private firms (where insurance is less standardized and more directly shapes the director/observer decision) is unstudied.

6. **Antitrust treatment of board observers.** No academic paper analyzes whether/how board observer arrangements should be treated under Clayton Act Section 8. The 2025 DOJ/FTC statement (if confirmed) is a regulatory development without academic analysis yet.

### Partial Gaps (Fragmented, Inconclusive)

7. **Advisory board effectiveness.** Some papers exist in management and family business journals, mostly conceptual or small-sample qualitative. No definitive empirical study.

8. **Cross-ownership comparison of governance architecture.** Some papers compare public and private, or family and non-family. No paper compares how the same governance roles function across VC, PE, family, public, and nonprofit contexts.

9. **Shadow director doctrine -- economic analysis.** Legal scholarship exists on when observers become shadow directors. Economic analysis of the shadow director boundary and its governance implications is nearly absent.

### This Paper's Position

This paper occupies the rightmost space on the frontier -- the region of genuinely unstudied phenomena. It is not incremental to any existing literature stream; it opens a new one. Specifically:

**Primary contribution:** First systematic documentation that private firm governance operates as a three-tier system (directors + observers + advisors) with distinct accountability structures across each tier.

**Theoretical contribution:** Introduces the "high-information, low-duty" principal class to agency theory. This extends the Adams & Ferreira (2007) monitoring-advising framework by showing that practice has evolved an institutional solution to the tradeoff they identify: separate the functions across different tiers rather than trying to balance them within a single board.

**Empirical contribution:** Practitioner interviews provide evidence that cannot be obtained from archival data -- tacit knowledge about how observers and advisors actually function, how liability shapes role selection, and how board dynamics change as observer counts grow.

## Potential Concerns a Referee May Raise

1. **"This is just about VC board seats."** Response: The interviews show the phenomenon extends to PE, family firms, and (per Fubini) public and nonprofit boards. The three-tier system is not unique to VC.

2. **"Three interviews is not enough."** Response: The paper is exploratory/theory-building (Eisenhardt 1989). Three interviews with differently-positioned practitioners (VC/PE investor, VC founder, multi-board director) provide triangulation across perspectives. The paper explicitly positions the interview findings as generating testable propositions for future quantitative research.

3. **"Why not use archival data?"** Response: Observer and advisor roles are not systematically disclosed in public filings. The phenomenon is tacit and relational -- it requires qualitative methods to surface. Future quantitative research (using VC contract data or hand-collected proxy statement data) could follow.

4. **"How is this different from the independent director literature?"** Response: Independent directors are voting members of the board with fiduciary duties. Observers have information access but no vote and no fiduciary duty. Advisors have influence but no formal governance role. These are structurally different roles with different accountability implications.

5. **"What's the corporate accountability contribution?"** Response: The accountability gap is the core finding: the parties with the most information (observers) may have the least responsibility, while the parties with formal duties (directors) may face conflicts that prevent effective monitoring. This has direct implications for investor protection, regulatory design, and board governance standards.
