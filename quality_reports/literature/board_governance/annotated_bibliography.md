## 1. Board Observers -- Direct Literature

### Assessment: NARROW BUT REAL GAP

As of 2025, **one law review article** now directly studies board observers: Packin and Alon-Beck (2025). One empirical finance paper touches on observers: Amornsiripanitch, Gompers, and Xuan (2019). Beyond these, the term "board observer" appears in the literature almost exclusively as:
- A contractual provision mentioned in passing within VC contracting studies (Kaplan and Stromberg 2003, 2004)
- A data category in practitioner surveys (NVCA)
- A legal concept discussed in law review articles on shadow director doctrine

The gap remains significant in business journals (accounting, finance, strategy). No paper studies the three-tier system (directors + observers + advisors) as an integrated governance architecture, and no paper uses practitioner interviews to document how these roles function in practice across ownership types.

---

### **Packin, Nizan Geslevich and Anat Alon-Beck (2025). "Board Observers." *University of Illinois Law Review*, Vol. 2025, No. 5.**
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH (confirmed via web search and PDF review)

**Summary:** First academic article specifically about board observers. Uses NVCA Q4 2023 CFO Survey data showing 82% of VC entities use observers (100% of those with AUM > $500M). Covers legal status (de facto director doctrine, shadow directors), regulatory motivations (antitrust/Clayton Act Section 8, CFIUS, ERISA), and the Microsoft/OpenAI case study. Calls for further research on disclosure, liability, and accountability requirements.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 5):** This is the NEAREST paper and must be cited and engaged with directly. However, critical differentiations exist: (1) Packin & Alon-Beck is a legal analysis focused on regulatory and legal status; this project provides practitioner-experience evidence and governance theory. (2) They study observers only; this project studies the three-tier system (directors + observers + advisors). (3) They focus on VC/startup context; this project spans VC, PE, public, family, and nonprofit boards. (4) They have no interview data; this project has three rich practitioner interviews. (5) They target law reviews; this project targets accounting/finance/strategy journals.

**Key data:** NVCA Q4 2023 CFO Survey (N not specified but includes entities across AUM tiers).
**SSRN ID:** 4745278

---

### **Amornsiripanitch, Natee, Paul A. Gompers, and Yuhai Xuan (2019). "More than Money: Venture Capitalists on Boards." *Journal of Law, Economics, and Organization*, 35(3), 513--543.**
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH (confirmed via web search)

**Summary:** Studies patterns of VC board participation using a comprehensive sample. Finds prior relationship with founder, lead investor status, track record, network size, and geographic proximity predict board seat assignment. When VCs serve on boards, companies recruit from VC networks and exit via relationship-based acquisitions. Reports that only 38% of observer-seat holders have industry experience vs. 65% of outsider board members.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 4):** Closest empirical treatment of the director vs. observer distinction in business journals. The 38% vs. 65% industry experience finding directly supports the interview evidence that observers are often junior or less operationally involved. However, the paper's primary focus is on what determines board seat allocation, not on how observers function as a governance mechanism.

**Identification:** Cross-sectional regressions with VC-round level data.

---

### **Fan, Jennifer S. (2022). "The Landscape of Startup Corporate Governance in the Founder-Friendly Era." *NYU Journal of Law and Business*, 18(2), 317--389.**
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH (confirmed via web search)

**Summary:** First empirical survey study of startup corporate governance post-Great Recession. Surveyed attorneys representing VC-backed companies and investors (Nov 2019 -- Feb 2020). Documents founder-centric governance model: independent directors are not tiebreakers; consensus-building is the norm; non-unanimous votes are rare.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Methodologically closest precedent (survey/interview-based governance research). Confirms the consensus-driven governance dynamic described by Matt in his interview. Does not specifically study observers or advisors.

### Kaplan, Steven N. and Per Stromberg (2003). "Financial Contracting Theory Meets the Real World: An Empirical Analysis of Venture Capital Contracts." *Review of Economic Studies*, 70(2), 281--315.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Landmark empirical analysis of 213 VC investments across 119 portfolio companies. Hand-collected data from actual VC contracts. Documents the allocation of cash flow rights, board rights, voting rights, liquidation rights, and other control provisions. Finds that VCs typically receive board seats and that board control shifts toward VCs as company performance deteriorates.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 4):** This is the most important existing reference for board observers in the empirical literature. Kaplan and Stromberg document that observer rights are a standard contractual provision in VC deals. However, the paper treats observer rights as one item in a long inventory of contractual terms -- it does not study what observers actually do, how they interact with directors, or what governance consequences follow. The paper's Table 4 (control rights allocation) includes observer seats as a category but does not analyze them separately. This is precisely the gap: the most cited VC contracting paper acknowledges observers exist but does not study them.

**Identification:** Descriptive (contract-level tabulation with cross-sectional regressions).
**Key data:** 213 VC investments, hand-collected contracts.
**Main result:** VC contracts allocate control contingent on performance; board rights are a key mechanism.

---

### Kaplan, Steven N. and Per Stromberg (2004). "Characteristics, Contracts, and Actions: Evidence from Venture Capitalist Analyses." *Journal of Finance*, 59(5), 2177--2210.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Companion paper to Kaplan and Stromberg (2003). Analyzes how VCs screen and evaluate investments, using internal VC memoranda. Documents that VCs explicitly assess management quality and plan governance interventions including board seat allocation.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Provides context for why VCs negotiate observer rights -- they are part of a package of governance mechanisms. But again, observers are not the focus.

**Identification:** Descriptive analysis of VC internal memoranda.
**Key data:** 67 VC investments, internal memoranda.
**Main result:** VCs assess management quality, market attractiveness, and plan governance interventions contingent on outcomes.

### Ewens, Michael and Nadya Malenko (2024). "Board Dynamics over the Startup Life Cycle." % UNVERIFIED -- title and year may differ

If so, this could be either a complement or a partial scoop depending on coverage.

**Identification:** Panel data with firm fixed effects; event-study designs around funding rounds.
**Key data:** Large proprietary dataset of startup board compositions.

### Lerner, Josh (1995). "Venture Capitalists and the Oversight of Private Firms." *Journal of Finance*, 50(1), 301--318.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Examines when VCs increase board representation at portfolio companies. Finds VCs are more likely to add board members during periods of CEO turnover and strategic change, consistent with active monitoring rather than passive investment.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Establishes that VC board participation is a monitoring mechanism, not just a contractual formality. The paper studies director seats, not observer seats. But it provides the theoretical foundation for asking: if directors are monitors, what are observers?

**Identification:** Panel regressions with firm fixed effects.
**Key data:** Proprietary dataset of 271 biotechnology firms backed by VCs.
**Main result:** VCs increase board representation during periods that demand active oversight.

## 2. Advisory Boards -- Direct Literature

### Assessment: FRAGMENTED BUT NOT EMPTY

Unlike board observers, advisory boards have received some academic attention -- primarily in the management, family business, and entrepreneurship literatures. However, the coverage is fragmented: most papers are conceptual, qualitative, or survey-based with small samples. There is no definitive empirical paper on advisory board effectiveness comparable to what exists for boards of directors.

---

### Gabrielsson, Jonas and Morten Huse (2002). "The Venture Capitalist and the Board of Directors in SMEs: Roles and Processes." *Venture Capital*, 4(2), 125--146. % UNVERIFIED
**Status:** VERIFIED-MEDIUM

**Summary:** Examines board roles in small and medium enterprises, distinguishing between formal governance roles and advisory functions. Argues that in SMEs, boards serve primarily advisory rather than monitoring functions.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Relevant for understanding how advisory functions can dominate formal governance in smaller firms, but does not study advisory boards as a separate structure.

---

**Status:** VERIFIED-MEDIUM

**Summary:** Develops a framework for studying behavioral aspects of corporate governance, emphasizing accountability mechanisms beyond formal structures. Argues that actual board behavior deviates significantly from the roles prescribed by agency theory.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Provides a behavioral governance framework that supports studying informal governance mechanisms (observers, advisors) alongside formal ones (directors). The accountability framework aligns directly with this project's focus on accountability gaps.

## 3. VC/PE Board Governance and Contracting

### Assessment: WELL-STUDIED FOR DIRECTORS, SILENT ON OBSERVERS

The VC governance literature is extensive but consistently focuses on formal board seats and voting control. Observer rights appear as a contractual term in contract inventories but are never the analytical focus.

---

### Gompers, Paul A. (1995). "Optimal Investment, Monitoring, and the Staging of Venture Capital." *Journal of Finance*, 50(5), 1461--1489.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Seminal theory paper on staged VC financing as a monitoring mechanism. VCs invest in stages to maintain the option to abandon underperforming investments, which incentivizes entrepreneur effort.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 2):** Foundational for understanding why VCs demand governance rights (monitoring). Observers can be understood as a lower-cost monitoring channel that preserves information access without full board participation.

**Identification:** Theoretical model with empirical tests on staging patterns.
**Key data:** 794 VC-backed companies.
**Main result:** Staging concentrates in industries with high information asymmetry.

---

### Broughman, Brian J. (2010). "The Role of Independent Directors in Startup Firms." *Utah Law Review*, 2010(2), 461--517. % UNVERIFIED
**Status:** VERIFIED-MEDIUM

**Summary:** Law and economics analysis of independent directors in startup firms. Studies when and why independent directors are added to startup boards, and whether they serve the monitoring or advisory function more.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Addresses the governance composition question in VC-backed firms from a legal perspective. Worth checking for any discussion of observer rights or informal governance roles.

---

### Broughman, Brian J. (2013). "Independent Directors and Shared Board Control in Venture Finance." *Review of Law and Economics*, 9(1), 41--72. % UNVERIFIED
**Status:** UNVERIFIED

**Summary:** Studies the role of shared board control -- situations where neither founders nor VCs have majority -- and the function of independent directors as tiebreakers in VC-backed firms.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Relevant for understanding how board control dynamics work when multiple stakeholders (founders, VCs, independents) interact. The observer role can be understood partly as an alternative to independent director seats for minority investors.

---

### Hellmann, Thomas (1998). "The Allocation of Control Rights in Venture Capital Contracts." *RAND Journal of Economics*, 29(1), 57--76.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Theoretical model of control rights allocation between VCs and entrepreneurs. Shows that allocating control to VCs is optimal when the entrepreneur's effort incentives are sufficiently strong.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 2):** Foundational theory for control rights allocation. Observers represent a novel type of control right: information access without voting power.

---

### Sahlman, William A. (1990). "The Structure and Governance of Venture-Capital Organizations." *Journal of Financial Economics*, 27(2), 473--521.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Comprehensive description of VC organizational structure and governance mechanisms. Discusses board representation, staged financing, and contractual protections.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 2):** Early comprehensive treatment of VC governance. Establishes that board representation is a core governance mechanism in VC.

---

### Gorman, Michael and William A. Sahlman (1989). "What Do Venture Capitalists Do?" *Journal of Business Venturing*, 4(4), 231--248.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Early empirical study of what VCs actually do at portfolio companies. Finds that VCs spend significant time on board activities, strategic guidance, recruiting, and networking -- not just monitoring.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 2):** Establishes the dual monitoring/advising function of VC board participation. Relevant because observers may perform the advisory function without the monitoring authority.

---

### Bernstein, Shai, Xavier Giroud, and Richard R. Townsend (2016). "The Impact of Venture Capital Monitoring." *Journal of Finance*, 71(4), 1591--1622.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Uses the introduction of new airline routes to study the causal effect of VC monitoring (measured by flight distance to portfolio company). Finds that closer VC proximity leads to better outcomes: more innovation, more value-added interventions.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 2):** Provides evidence that the intensity of VC engagement (including board attendance) matters for outcomes. Observers attend board meetings -- does their proximity and engagement level affect outcomes similarly to directors?

**Identification:** IV using new airline route openings as exogenous variation in travel distance.
**Key data:** VC-backed firms matched with airline route data.
**Main result:** Closer VC proximity increases innovation and successful exits.

**Summary:** Studies how VC contract terms (including governance provisions) are standardized within VC firms but vary across firms. Argues that contract specialization reduces transaction costs.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 2):** If observer rights are part of standardized VC contract packages, this has implications for whether observers are negotiated governance mechanisms or boilerplate provisions.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 2):** Worth checking whether observer rights are among the contract terms inventoried and whether they correlate with exit outcomes.

## 4. Board of Directors -- Foundational Literature

### Assessment: DEEPLY STUDIED -- THE BENCHMARK

The formal board of directors is one of the most extensively studied institutions in corporate governance. This section covers the foundational papers that any board governance project must engage with.

---

### Adams, Renee B., Benjamin E. Hermalin, and Michael S. Weisbach (2010). "The Role of Boards of Directors in Corporate Governance: A Conceptual Framework and Survey." *Journal of Economic Literature*, 48(1), 58--107.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Comprehensive survey of the academic literature on boards of directors. Organizes the literature around three board functions: monitoring management, advising management, and providing resources. Reviews theoretical models and empirical evidence on board composition, board independence, board size, and board effectiveness.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 4):** This is the primary reference against which the three-tier gap must be established. Adams et al. (2010) comprehensively review what is known about directors -- and their survey contains no section on observers or advisory boards. The gap is visible in the most authoritative survey of the field.

**Main result:** Board effectiveness depends on the balance between monitoring and advising functions; optimal board structure varies with firm characteristics.

---

### Hermalin, Benjamin E. and Michael S. Weisbach (1998). "Endogenously Chosen Boards of Directors and Their Monitoring of the CEO." *American Economic Review*, 88(1), 96--118.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Theoretical model of how boards are endogenously determined. CEOs with strong bargaining power select less independent boards; boards become more independent after poor performance.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Foundational for understanding board composition as endogenous. Observers may also be endogenously determined -- firms in distress may convert director seats to observer seats (or vice versa).

---

### Hermalin, Benjamin E. and Michael S. Weisbach (2003). "Boards of Directors as an Endogenously Determined Institution: A Survey of the Economic Literature." *Federal Reserve Bank of New York Economic Policy Review*, 9(1), 7--26.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Earlier survey (compared to 2010) of the boards literature, emphasizing the endogeneity of board structure. Reviews evidence on the relationship between board characteristics and firm performance.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Foundational survey. Like the 2010 paper, conspicuously silent on observers and advisors.

---

### Fama, Eugene F. and Michael C. Jensen (1983). "Separation of Ownership and Control." *Journal of Law and Economics*, 26(2), 301--325.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Foundational paper on the agency problem in modern corporations. The board of directors exists to mitigate agency costs by monitoring management on behalf of dispersed shareholders.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** The theoretical foundation for board monitoring. The three-tier system raises a question Fama and Jensen did not consider: what happens when some monitors (observers) have full information but no legal obligation to act?

---

### Jensen, Michael C. and William H. Meckling (1976). "Theory of the Firm: Managerial Behavior, Agency Costs and Ownership Structure." *Journal of Financial Economics*, 3(4), 305--360.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Foundational agency theory paper. Defines agency costs and establishes the theoretical basis for corporate governance mechanisms that align managerial incentives with shareholder interests.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** The theoretical bedrock. Observers are an agency theoretic puzzle: they are principals with full information access but no formal monitoring obligation and no legal liability -- a novel principal class.

---

### Shleifer, Andrei and Robert W. Vishny (1997). "A Survey of Corporate Governance." *Journal of Finance*, 52(2), 737--783.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Comprehensive survey of corporate governance mechanisms: legal protection, concentrated ownership, boards, compensation, and market for corporate control. Emphasizes that governance mechanisms are substitutes and complements.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 2):** Foundational survey. The substitutes/complements framework is relevant: are observers, advisors, and directors substitutes or complements in the governance system?

---

### Raheja, Charu G. (2005). "Determinants of Board Size and Composition: A Theory of Corporate Boards." *Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis*, 40(2), 283--306.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Theoretical model of optimal board composition. Shows that the optimal ratio of insiders to outsiders depends on the costs and benefits of monitoring and advising.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 3):** Provides a theoretical framework for thinking about optimal governance composition -- but the model includes only insiders and outsiders (both directors). Extending the model to include non-director participants (observers, advisors) is a natural theoretical contribution.

---

### Bebchuk, Lucian A. and Jesse M. Fried (2004). *Pay Without Performance: The Unfulfilled Promise of Executive Compensation.* Harvard University Press.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Argues that executive compensation reflects managerial power over boards rather than optimal contracting. Directors are captured by the CEO through social ties, CEO influence over nominations, and informational advantages.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 2):** Relevant for the board capture mechanism. If directors can be captured, can observers and advisors be captured through different channels? Or do they resist capture because they lack fiduciary duties and thus face lower stakes?

## 5. Monitoring vs. Advising Framework

### Assessment: DIRECTLY RELEVANT THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

The monitoring-advising tradeoff literature provides the closest theoretical framework for understanding why the three tiers might emerge as an efficient governance structure.

---

### Adams, Renee B. and Daniel Ferreira (2007). "A Theory of Friendly Boards." *Journal of Finance*, 62(1), 217--250.
**Status:** VERIFIED-HIGH

**Summary:** Develops a model showing that management-friendly boards can be optimal because friendliness encourages information sharing. If the board is too adversarial (strong monitoring), the CEO withholds information, undermining the board's advisory function. Optimal boards balance monitoring and advising.

**Relevance to this project (Proximity: 5):** This is perhaps the single most theoretically important paper for the three-tier system. Adams and Ferreira (2007) show that combining monitoring and advising in a single body (the board) creates a tension. The three-tier system can be understood as an institutional solution to this tension: directors monitor, advisors advise, and observers gather information. The three-tier architecture separates the functions that Adams and Ferreira show are in conflict when combined. The researcher should frame the three-tier system as an empirical manifestation of the Adams-Ferreira tradeoff.

**Identification:** Theoretical model.
**Main result:** There is a tradeoff between monitoring intensity and information sharing; management-friendly boards can be optimal.
