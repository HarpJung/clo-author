  ABSTRACT  
 
Power dynamics and governance in venture capital (VC) and startups are evolving, with innovative 
mechanisms balancing influence and control. One key tool is the appointment of board observers—
individuals without voting rights who serve as strategic eyes on the board, providing insight and influence 
without the fiduciary responsibilities of board membership. Their role exemplifies control without formal 
control, enabling investors to closely monitor and guide governance, strategy, and performance. 
 
This Article is the first to explore the role of board observers in VC and startups. These observers act as 
intermediaries, bridging active investors and corporate boards and aligning with shared strategic goals. The 
Article highlights investors' motivations for this engagement, including enhanced governance, access to 
influence, risk mitigation, and compliance with complex regulations like antitrust, CFIUS, and ERISA. 
 
Board observers drew attention in November 2023 when Microsoft was appointed as a non-voting observer 
during OpenAI’s internal discord and CEO removal, spotlighting their impact. Regulatory scrutiny 
followed, leading Microsoft to step down in July 2024, underscoring the role of corporate VC in governance 
and strategic direction. 
 
The Article contributes new insights by tracing board observers’ emergence, revealing results from the 
NVCA’s Q4 2023 CFO Survey showing 82% of VC entities use observers, with all entities over $500 
million in assets under management adopting this model. It examines the legal status, rights, and duties of 
board observers, their role in deal negotiations, and their regulatory impact. It suggests further work to 
define disclosure, liability, and accountability requirements for board observers, examining issues like 
startup boards' consensus voting, supervision focus, and the chilling effect on traditional members.

This Article offers a groundbreaking examination of the board observer role, filling a 
significant gap in both legal and business scholarship. It highlights how the emergence of board 
observer positions reflects the evolving strategies of investor activism to achieve more effective 
corporate governance and influence boardroom decisions, while also impacting the competitive 
landscape and allowing investors to navigate and sometimes avoid stringent regulatory 
requirements. The rise of board observers marks a shift in how investors interact with and influence 
companies. These positions allow investors to gain critical insights and exert subtle yet impactful 
influence without assuming the formal responsibilities and liabilities of board membership. This 
strategic tool has become increasingly popular as investors seek to navigate the intricate landscape 
of corporate governance and regulatory compliance more effectively.4  
Furthermore, the Article explores the potential for conflicts of interest, particularly in 
evaluating corporate transactions where governance, regulatory compliance, antitrust issues, and 
national security concerns intersect. The board observer role, while beneficial for investors by 
allowing them to gain insights and exert influence without assuming formal control, also offers 
advantages for entrepreneurs and founders by enabling them to retain more control and receive 
additional guidance, presenting challenges that require careful consideration and management to 
maintain the delicate balance between influence and oversight.  By shedding light on the board 
observer phenomenon, the Article emphasizes the need for ongoing research and dialogue. It calls 
for a nuanced understanding of the implications and efficacy of board observers in corporate 
governance. This exploration is vital for developing more robust frameworks that address the 
evolving needs of investors and the companies they support.5

The decision to grant Microsoft a board observer seat in OpenAI was, therefore, seen as a way to 
strengthen the partnership between the two entities, as it ensures that Microsoft’s interests are 
considered in OpenAI’s strategic decisions and allows for closer collaboration. The appointment 
of Dee Templeton, a 25-year veteran at Microsoft and head of Technology and Research 
Partnerships and Operations, as a non-voting observer on OpenAI's board, illustrated the 
complexities of corporate governance in the tech sector during its rapid growth.11 Her appointment 
aligned with a tumultuous phase within OpenAI, which was characterized by the incident 
involving the rehiring of OpenAI’s CEO, raising significant questions about the autonomy of 
OpenAI in the shadow of Microsoft’s corporate reach, and underscoring Microsoft's close, yet 
nuanced involvement in OpenAI's strategic course without overt interference. How could OpenAI 
maintain its autonomy in the partnership with Microsoft? This has been the main question that 
arose in January 2024, after news broke that the Department of Justice (DOJ) and the Federal 
Trade Commission (FTC) started examining the evolving relationship between the two as a focal 
point of antitrust scrutiny, underscoring a complex interplay of control, competition, and 
regulatory concerns within the AI sector.12  The tightness and complexity of the relationship were 
also demonstrated by Microsoft’s substantial financial investment in and integration of OpenAI’s 
advanced technologies into its core products, notably Bing. However, the key issue at the heart of 
the DOJ and FTC’s scrutiny has been the degree of Microsoft’s influence over OpenAI, that was 
particularly highlighted by the incident involving the rehiring of OpenAI’s CEO, which raised 
significant questions about the autonomy of OpenAI in the shadow of Microsoft’s corporate 
reach.13 Finally, the relationship has intensified regulatory concerns regarding the potential 
monopolization of the AI market, specifically in the realm of large language models (LLMs). This 
potential for market dominance by Microsoft through OpenAI poses a threat to the competitive 
landscape, potentially stifling innovation and maintaining barriers to entry. Indeed, Microsoft’s 
close relationship with OpenAI, especially in light of its ongoing investment in OpenAI’s 
competitors, could also be indicative of the growing trend of tech giants investing heavily in 
emerging technologies and seeking greater involvement in governance and strategic direction of 
their investee companies, as well as in the overall market in which their products compete.14

the NVCA Survey findings reveal a direct correlation between the size of an entity's Assets Under

A. 
Identifying the Potential Participants and Investor Activists in Startup Governance 
and Funding 
The first set of participants is the entrepreneurs that are founding the startups. Startups 
usually begin their journey primarily with internal funding. Outside investors, often nicknamed in 
the early stages as the three Fs (friends, families and fools), in later funding stages include various 
entities. Nevertheless, banks, typically do not lend to startup companies in their early stages. This 
is mainly due to their lack of a track record and tangible assets.54 Instead, at the initial stages, the 
founders tend to use their own funds, and issue common equity to themselves.55 As the financing 
needs grow, founders often start seeking external sources of finance from angel investors.56 Angel 
investors often invest at earlier stages than VCs and in smaller amount and usually receive common 
stocks for their investments. And as the financing needs continue to grow, startups move to seek 
financing from VCs.  
This leads us to the second set of participants – VCs and AVCs. Starting with VCs, which 
are usually entities comprised of investment professionals that are structured as limited 
partnerships, these entities have general and limited partners (LPs) and provide financing to 
startups. Limited partners are the investors that provide capital to the VC, usually large 
endowments, pension funds, and banks.57 Before LPs decide to invest in a VC, the VC usually 
provides it with a memorandum describing the fund’s investment strategy, without disclosing the 
specific firms the fund will invest in. Although the LPs provide most of the capital, they are not 
involved in the ongoing management of the fund’s business and may be excluded from decisions, 
such as whether to approve investment in a particular company.58  Instead, it is the general partners 
who decide which companies to invest in, and they often also play an active role in the governance 
of these companies (e.g., by serving on their boards).59 Put simply, the LPs’ main contribution is 
capital, whereas the general partners’ main contribution is financial bootstrapping and expertise.60 
The compensation of general partners is composed of a relatively small annual management fee 
for its services, and a carried interest—i.e., a right to receive a pre-defined percentage of the profits 
realized by the partnership.61 The general partners receive carried interest when distributions are 
made to the LPs, and only after the LPs received their capital contributions back. With this 
description in mind, Figure 1 illustrates the structure of the VC fund.

BOARD OBSERVERS ON THE RISE  
Having understood who the participants in the startup ecosystem are, this Part turns to 
further explore the role that board observers play within the corporate governance of startups. 
Section A introduces the concept of board observers and discusses potential benefits and 
disadvantages associated with it. Section B presents and examines empirical data that reflects the

A. 
Intro: The Role of Board Observers 
The board observer role is an interesting and multifaceted one in the world of corporate 
governance, that offers many advantages. For instance, serving as a board observer enables 
individuals to gain valuable experience and knowledge about corporate governance, improving 
one’s understanding of a specific industry and company, and building relationships with board 
members as well as other potential stakeholders.98 Similarly, companies with board observers 
benefit from their objective contributions to the board’s decision-making process, offering insights 
into business operations, goals, and strategies. In the innovative startup ecosystem, particularly, 
young entrepreneurial companies with less experienced employees and management often 
genuinely need additional expertise, guidance, and mentorship during their early stages of growth. 
Board observers, often serving on behalf of venture capital firms, monitor the progress and 
performance of the companies in which they have invested. Having done so with other startups, 
these observers bring a wealth of industry-specific knowledge and experience, helping companies 
navigate complex challenges and make informed decisions. They also help startups avoid common 
pitfalls and mistakes that may occur during the early stages of growth.  Board observers provide 
valuable feedback to the board of directors, identifying areas for operational or strategic 
improvement. They also help ensure that the company complies with the terms of its investment 
agreement and meets investor expectations. Appointed by VC firms as a condition of their 
investment, board observers effectively have a say in the company’s decision-making process, 
ensuring that invested funds are used effectively. Additionally, board observers assess the 
company's progress and determine whether additional investment is warranted. 
On the other hand, there are clear disadvantages to having board observers. For those who 
use board observers on their behalf, the main drawbacks include the lack of decision-making power 
– the voting power – to actually actively decide on matters the board is addressing, and the potential 
for limited access to information.99  Indeed, observers are held to a confidentiality obligation and

Second, the issue of conflict of interest is more common and significant than it may initially 
seem, since board observers are subject to many agency-related problems and corporate 
governance-known complexities. Board observers serve on behalf of companies’ – often strategic 
– investors that have different interests, goals, and priorities than those of the companies. Some 
illustrations of this include the following situations: (i) Biases reflected in influencing decision-
making. Board observers may prioritize the interests of an investor over the interests of the 
startup/company they are involved with. For instance, the decision to include Microsoft as a board 
observer at OpenAI presented such a challenge, given its potential to allow Microsoft to 
significantly influence OpenAI’s strategic direction in a way that favors its own business interests, 
potentially distorting market competition. Similarly, or relatedly, board observers might observe 
boards of multiple entities, including potential competing businesses that are associated with the 
venture capital firm. This would lead to biased decision-making, where the board observer’s 
decisions are influenced by the interests of the venture capital firm rather than the best interests of 
the startup or company. (ii) Limited disclosure. Board observers may not disclose all relevant 
information to the company they are involved with, especially if that information conflicts with 
the interests of the investor or VC firm they are associated with. This can lead to the startup or 
company making uninformed decisions that may negatively impact their growth and development. 
(iii) Lack of objectivity. Board observers may lack objectivity when evaluating the performance 
of the company they are involved with. This can lead to unrealistic expectations, pressure to 
achieve unrealistic goals, and ultimately, a negative impact on the company’s growth and 
development. (iv) Toxic, non-collaborative, or unproductive environments. Conflict of interest can 
result in hostile behavior and strain the relationship between the board observer, the VC firm, and 
the startup company. This can lead to a breakdown in communication, a lack of trust, and negative 
impacts on the startup’s growth and potential success.

IV. 
THE LEGAL STATE OF BOARD OBSERVERS 
As described above, board observers are unique in their role and responsibilities. Typically 
appointed through contractual instruments such as investment agreements, term sheets, or side 
letters—though rarely mentioned in bylaws or articles of association—board observers are not 
entitled to vote but are typically entitled to attend board meetings and receive board papers. In 
addition, they can actively participate in and contribute to board conversations, which, based on 
informal conversations with practitioners and directors, as well as findings in psychology 
literature, can impact the boardroom dynamic, potentially causing a chilling effect.110 Moreover, 
the presence and participation of board observers can impact corporate decision-making processes, 
similar to bargaining in the shadow of the law, where board decisions are made under observation. 
Focusing on the legal status of board observers, and how it differs from traditional board members, 
this part explores their rights, duties, and liability in the U.S. and compares them to other 
jurisdictions, offering some analogies to similar roles.

A. 
Board Observers as Directors? 
In the context of business organizations, a director is defined as “[a] member of a board 
appointed to direct or manage the affairs of a commercial corporation or company.”111 Board 
members manage a business organization’s affairs by: “(1) selecting senior officers; (2) controlling 
executive compensation; (3) delegating administrative authority to officers; (4) making high level 
corporate policy; (5) deciding financing and capital allocation; and (6) supervising the ‘welfare of 
the whole enterprise.’”112 This formal power to direct and manage a business organization is 
accompanied by certain responsibilities and duties.113 Directors owe a duty of care and loyalty to 
shareholders and can be voted out by shareholders for reasons such as self-dealing, 
mismanagement or even for no reason.114  
Reviewing the methods by which board observers manage a business organization’s 
affairs, it is evident that they have fewer responsibilities, duties, and legal liabilities compared to 
traditional board directors. However, the role of a board observer and that of a traditional board 
member bear some resemblance, as described in the prior parts of this Article. In many 
jurisdictions, including the U.S., the resemblance in functions, the conduct of board observers, and 
certain behaviors on their part can result in them being considered as what is known as de facto 
directors, a term established in case law.115 However, there are other legal classifications that could 
suggest the types of duties and liabilities expected of board observers. For instance, in some 
jurisdictions, such as the UK, board observers can arguably be classified as shadow directors, a 
notion defined in existing statutes.116 This Section explores the legal classification possibilities, 
examining how board observers can be viewed in the U.S., using some comparative law, and the 
implications of such classifications on their rights, duties, and liabilities.

may be named as performing similar functions to a director even if he or she does not possess the 
directors’ ‘formal power to direct and manage a corporation, and the responsibilities and duties 
that accompany those powers.’”127 According to the dissenting opinion, “a person named in the 
registration statement as being or about to become a person performing similar functions if, 
pursuant to this statement, he or she possesses powers or abilities that are of a like nature or kind 
as the power to direct or manage the affairs of the corporation.”128 Applying this theory to the facts, 
the dissenting opinion highlighted three elements, that were identified in the registration statement, 
to support the view that Zou and Downs possess powers or abilities that are of a like nature or kind 
as the power to directors: (i) they could significantly influence the outcome of matters submitted 
for board approval; (ii) they had access to information from board meetings (“eyes and ears at 
board meetings”); and (iii) they were entitled to the same payment as independent directors and 
were eligible for reimbursement for attending meetings of the board.129 
This Obasi case provides clear guidance that board observers are not liable as directors or 
'similar' persons under Section 11, even if they have access to inside information, attend board 
meetings, and can influence business decisions and other matters submitted to the board. However, 
it also implies that if board observers de facto act as directors—by having voting rights, tenures 
dependent on shareholders, or owing fiduciary duties to shareholders—they could indeed be held 
liable as such. Additionally, it is worth noting the dissenting opinion's broader approach suggests 
that many board observers could argueably be held liable under Section 11, regardless of whether 
they have formal voting rights or owe fiduciary duties.  
In addition to the U.S., other juridiscitions such as the UK also recognize the de facto 
directors concept. In particular, UK laws define three types of directors, and one of those categories 
is the de facto directors. Indeed, according to section 250 of the Companies Act 2006 (CA), as 
stated in the explanatory notes, the definition of directors covers a de facto director, which is 
defined as “a person who has assumed the status and functions of a company director even though 
he has not been properly appointed.”130

V. 
THE ALLURE OF BOARD OBSERVERS FOR SAVVY INVESTORS    
Throughout their life cycle, businesses, particularly startup companies, face significant 
capital challenges and often turn to investors for external funding. A crucial aspect of these funding 
agreements is the negotiation of the board's composition. Investors seek board seats for various 
reasons, including gaining control, accessing information, and monitoring the companies' 
activities.145 Entrepreneurs typically aim to maintain control while securing the external capital 
needed to scale their growth and sustain their leadership positions.146  Usually, the board 
composition evolves over time, starting with founder-controlled boards and gradually shifting to 
investor-controlled boards as investors demand more seats.147  In recent years, however, investors 
have begun to diverge from the industry norm of negotiating the majority of board seats and have 
increasingly relied on board observers.148  This shift might have at least been partially characterized 
by asking for, or negotiating to get, non-voting board observer seats instead. Following the 
previous Section, which explores the characteristics of  board observers as an innovative corporate 
governance rule, how popular these seats have become, and their legal status, this Part continues 
by focusing on the allure of board observers for investors. Apecifically, it examines the various 
incentives for investors associated with using board observers. Section A zooms in on general 
governance considerations. Section B dives into matters associated with minimizing liability 
exposure. Section C concentrates on antitrust issues. Section D explores concerns related to CFIUS 
regulation. Finally, Section E delves into ERISA-related regulatory matters. 
A. 
General Governance Considerations 
Entrepreneurs and investors might be interested in board observer seats for various 
governance-related reasons. From the perspective of investors, observer seats may be used to gain 
access to information or to have an influence over business and governance decisions of the 
company without having to comply with the same legal obligations and duties applying to board 
directors (e.g., fiduciary duties). In the context of startups, the option of board observers might be 
particularly appealing for minority shareholders, who join at a late round with a funding amount 
that cannot justify a board member seat.149 It could similarly appeal to seed and pre-seed investors, 
as exercising or securing extensive investor controls would typically make little economic sense 
at the startup’s very early, research and development (R&D)-focused stage.150  Finally, certain 
investors may prefer negotiating for board observers, rather than board seats, as a general corporate 
policy matter.151 Notably, as a matter of policy, many CVCs (apparently, most of them) either 
never lead investment rounds, or have a strong preference to joining them as followers. One direct

B. 
Minimizing Liability Exposure  
Investors might also be interested in negotiating for board observer seats, as opposed to 
traditional board member seats, to minimize potential liability exposure, and litigation risks.154 
While the potential legal liability of board observers under existing legal frameworks is not yet 
fully developed,155 it is safe to argue, certainly after the 2020 changes mentioned above, that it is 
generally lower than the potential liability applying to actual board members.156 To start, board 
observers are generally not subject to the same fiduciary duties applying to board members.157 This 
means that observers would generally not be directly liable in litigation concerning the breach of 
fiduciary duties owed to shareholders. The liability of board observers under securities laws may 
also be more limited relative to that of board members. For example, in Obasi, the U.S. Court of 
Appeals for the Third Circuit ruled that, because of the board observers’ limited authority and 
obligations, they were not held liable for misrepresentations made by their company under Section 
11 of the Securities Act of 1933.158 While it is still unclear whether and to what extend this 
securities regulation case can be generalized and applicable to other contexts in which board 
observers operate, the litigation exposure of observers, at least under federal securities laws, 
appears to be lower in comparison to that of traditional board members.159

responsibilities and liabilities as traditional board members—leading to the initial conclusion that

board observers seeking inclusion in a company’s D&O insurance policy.160 This trend, however,

Passive Investments and Board Observers 
Some commentators believe that China poses a growing threat to the U.S. national security. 
Perhaps nowhere is that risk more acute than with the development of advanced technology. China 
has used advanced technology – much of it stolen from the U.S., some of it sent there through 
corporate outsourcing – to ramp up its military modernization and build a mass surveillance state 
that commits deplorable human rights violations, including the detention of millions of Uighurs in 
Xinjiang. Addressing these threats from the Chinese government, and improving the U.S.’ 
domestic capability to compete with China, have been a top priority of the U.S. Senate Committee 
on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs, Full Committee Hearing in the 2020’s.227 
In the realm of national security and financial regulation, the pivotal roles of FIRRMA and 
CFIUS in scrutinizing foreign investments, especially in the Technology, Infrastructure, and Data 
(TID) sectors, have been notably expanded. The focus now encompasses not just direct 
investments but also passive ones, where foreign entities may lack overt control. Passive 
investments, traditionally less scrutinized, are now re-characterized under FIRRMA to include 
investments where foreign entities may not have a substantial stake (less than 10% interest) or 
direct control. This redefinition ensures a more comprehensive review of foreign investments, 
capturing scenarios where even minimal stakes could pose a national security risk. This enhanced 
scrutiny is critical in an era where technological advancements and data security are paramount to 
national security.228 The changes in the treatment of passive investments underscore the evolving 
landscape of financial transactions and the increasing complexity of corporate structures in the 
global economy. By expanding the definition of what constitutes a passive investment, FIRRMA 
and CFIUS effectively close loopholes that previously allowed certain foreign investments to 
escape thorough scrutiny. This proactive approach is essential in safeguarding sensitive U.S.

investor and a company in which the investor holds a stake, and confers specific rights on the 
investor, enabling their involvement in the governance and operational aspects of the company.

simultaneously circumventing certain regulatory hurdles associated with ERISA. The primary 
utility of management rights letters lies in their ability to confer specified rights upon investors. 
These semi-board observer type of rights typically represent a tangible extension of the investor’s 
influence within the operational mechanics of the company.240 For VC and PE investors, these 
rights are more than just tools for oversight; they are important levers for guiding strategic 
directions and safeguarding their investments.241 Legally, the significance of management rights

comprehensive set of rules and fiduciary obligations, casts a wide net over investment activities,

VI. 
PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS AND FUTURE RESEARCH 
The impact of board observers on boardroom discussions is multifaceted and significant. 
When an observer is present, the dynamic in the boardroom can change considerably. Indeed, 
interviews with directors,244 and insights from psychology literature reveal that the mere presence 
of an observer can impact decision-making processes, and therefore warrants further investigation. 
Specifically, the Hawthorne Effect, a well-established concept, suggests that individuals improve 
their performance when they know they are being observed.245 This phenomenon was first 
observed in the 1920s and 1930s during experiments at the Western Electric Hawthorne Works, 
where changes in work conditions, led to increased productivity merely because workers knew 
they were being watched.246  Similarly, psychological effects of being watched by others have been 
proven a powerful tool in boosting honest or charitable behaviors,247 and enhancing cooperation 
in real-world settings.248 Likewise, this watching effect increases self-awareness,249 and causes 
individuals to consciously modify their behavior to increase compliance with social standards.250

information, and the perceptions of those changes by the individuals directly affected by them.”252 
Similarly, a systematic review and meta-analysis of 91 studies questions the validity of a watching 
eyes effect, finding only a small significant influence of eye cues on behavior and highlighting 
inconsistencies across various moderating factors.253  Given this mixed evidence, further research 
is essential to understand the nuanced impact of observers in the boardroom. Investigating this 
could reveal whether increased self-awareness and adherence to norms due to being watched 
translate into better decision-making and governance at the highest levels of corporate 
management. Such insights could potentially lead to more effective governance practices and 
policies, ensuring that board members' behavior aligns with the best interests of stakeholders and 
the broader community.  
Fiduciary duties, or the lack thereof, for board observers in the U.S. represent a critical 
issue that warrants further research. Currently, board observers do not owe fiduciary duties to the 
company or its shareholders, which raises significant concerns. These observers have access to 
crucial information and can influence company decisions, yet they are not bound by the same legal 
obligations as directors. It is not yet obvious what type of accountability, liability, and legal 
requirements should be associated with the board observer role. Future research must include an 
expanded analysis of how such liabilities should be assigned. Perhaps a sliding scale of 
accountability or legal liability based on the nature of the role, exposure, etc., could be developed. 
Alternatively, there could be clear and mandatory disclosure requirements as implied by the FTC 
in certain situations like mergers. Addressing these concerns is essential to ensure board observers 
do not exploit their positions to the disadvantage of the company and its stakeholders. 
When researching the legal duties of a director and determining what these should 
encompass, it is crucial to understand if the fact that board observers do not vote —unlike 
traditional board members—actually makes a difference in the real world. While formal votes do 
occur in board meetings, no empirical data specifically detailing the frequency of formal votes in 
board meetings is available.  Moreover, commentators have argued that boards generally aim for 
consensus,254 and if that is the case many decisions can be made collaboratively without formal 
voting.255 More specifically, a study focusing on Israeli companies with significant government

In addition, a crowding-out effect is another issue that should be further explored, as the 
use of board observers can potentially crowd-out more meaningful investor activism, regulation,

investor pressures that might otherwise benefit the company. Additionally, it may allow bad actors 
who would otherwise have been directors to avoid liability, as observers do not face similar risks. 
On the regulatory side, the possibility of appointing board observers might reduce the perceived 
need for other mechanisms to regulate or oversee private companies. Regulatory efforts might 
diminish if board observers are seen as sufficient monitors, which could undermine comprehensive 
oversight.

contractual agreements such as NDAs, and the exclusion of observers to protect trade secrets or
