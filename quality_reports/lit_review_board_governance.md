## Executive Summary

This literature review systematically examines the academic landscape around the three-tier board governance architecture (directors, observers, advisors). The review covers finance, accounting, management, law, and entrepreneurship literatures.

**Bottom line: The gap is real, though narrower than initially assessed.**

- **Board observers:** One law review article now exists: **Packin & Alon-Beck (2025)**, "Board Observers," *University of Illinois Law Review*, Vol. 2025, No. 5 (SSRN: 4745278). This is a legal analysis of observer status, regulation, and antitrust risk. **Amornsiripanitch, Gompers & Xuan (JLEO, 2019)** report observer industry experience data as a secondary finding (38% vs 65% for directors). However, **no accounting, finance, or strategy journal paper** studies observers. The practitioner experience and governance-theoretic implications remain undocumented in business scholarship.
- **Advisory boards:** A handful of papers exist, mostly in management and family business journals, mostly conceptual or qualitative with small samples. No definitive empirical treatment.
- **Three-tier system:** Zero papers -- including Packin & Alon-Beck -- study the integrated system (directors + observers + advisors). This remains a genuinely novel contribution.
- **Scooping risk:** Low. Packin & Alon-Beck is complementary (legal analysis) not competitive (governance theory with practitioner data). Ewens and Malenko (2024) should still be checked for observer-level data.

## Findings by Topic

### 1. Board Observers: The Core Gap (Confirmed)

**Finding: No empirical paper exists with board observers as the primary research question.**

The most relevant existing reference is Kaplan and Stromberg (2003, RES), which inventories VC contract terms including observer rights. Their Table 4 includes observer seats as one of many contractual provisions. The paper does not analyze observer behavior, observer effects on governance, or why observer status is chosen over director status.

Ewens and Malenko (2024) study startup board dynamics over the firm lifecycle. This is the nearest potential competitor. The paper focuses on director seats and how control shifts between founders, VCs, and independents. The researcher should verify whether their dataset includes observer-level data and whether any supplementary analysis addresses observers. If observers are in the data but not analyzed, this is a strong complement. If observers are analyzed, the gap narrows but the qualitative approach remains differentiated.

Other VC governance papers (Lerner 1995; Hellmann and Puri 2002; Bernstein et al. 2016; Hochberg 2012) study VC monitoring and governance through director seats and never mention observers as a distinct analytical category.

**Assessment: The gap is real. This is the paper's strongest claim.**

### 2. Advisory Boards: Fragmented Literature (Partially Confirmed)

**Finding: Some papers exist, but no definitive empirical treatment.**

The advisory board literature is scattered across management, family business, and entrepreneurship journals. Key observations:

- Huse (2005) develops an accountability framework for behavioral governance that could accommodate advisory boards but does not study them empirically.
- The family business literature (Johannisson and Huse 2000; Schwartz and Barnes 1991; Aronoff and Ward 2011) treats advisory boards as governance mechanisms for family firms, usually as a stepping stone toward formal outside boards.
- The German Mittelstand literature discusses Beirate (advisory boards) as a formal governance layer distinct from the Aufsichtsrat (supervisory board), providing an international comparison point.
- Practitioner literature from NACD, law firms, and consulting firms is richer than academic literature.

**Assessment: The gap is partial -- literature exists but is fragmented, non-cumulative, and lacks a definitive empirical study. The paper should acknowledge existing work while noting its limitations.**

### 3. Three-Tier System: Complete Gap (Confirmed)

**Finding: No paper studies the three-tier system as an integrated governance architecture.**

The closest literature streams are:
- Comparative governance (Aguilera and Jackson 2010) discusses multi-tier board systems across countries (especially the German two-tier model) but does not study the informal US three-tier system.
- VC contracting (Kaplan and Stromberg 2003) documents that multiple governance mechanisms coexist but does not study their interaction.
- The monitoring-advising framework (Adams and Ferreira 2007) shows theoretically why separating functions might be valuable, but does not document the institutional form this separation takes.

**Assessment: This is a genuine gap. No paper studies how directors, observers, and advisors interact as a governance system.**

### 4. Shadow Director Doctrine: Legal Literature Exists, Economic Analysis Thin

**Finding: Legal scholarship on shadow directors exists (UK law); economic analysis is sparse.**

The UK Companies Act 2006, Section 251 provides the statutory definition. Legal scholarship discusses the threshold at which informal governance participation triggers fiduciary duties. However, law and economics analysis of how the shadow director doctrine affects governance behavior (e.g., does the risk of being classified as a shadow director discipline observer behavior?) is nearly absent.

Moore (2016) -- cited in the research spec -- needs verification. If confirmed, it provides the legal framework for the observer-director boundary.

**Assessment: The legal doctrine is established. The economic analysis of how it shapes governance behavior is missing.**

### 5. DOJ/FTC 2025 Developments: Cannot Verify

**Finding: I cannot confirm the 2025 DOJ/FTC statement on board observers and Clayton Act Section 8.**

I am aware that DOJ significantly increased Section 8 enforcement starting in 2022-2023, with several actions against interlocking directorates (including the Endeavor/UFC case and others). The extension to board observer arrangements would be a logical next step in this enforcement trend. However, I cannot confirm the specific 2025 statement cited in the research spec.

**Action required:** The researcher should search:
- DOJ Antitrust Division press releases and policy statements (2024-2025)
- FTC Commissioner speeches and statements
- Law360, Bloomberg Law, and antitrust law blogs for coverage
- Recent law review articles on Section 8 enforcement

**Assessment: If confirmed, this is extremely important for the paper -- it validates the premise that regulators view observers as governance participants, not passive attendees.**

### 6. Papers the Researcher May Have Missed

The following papers are not in the research spec but are directly relevant:

1. **Yermack (2004)** -- Reputation incentives for outside directors. Provides empirical evidence for the reputation mechanism Toj describes for observers.

2. **Masulis and Mobbs (2011)** -- Director heterogeneity. Shows that the director/observer distinction may be less sharp in practice than formal roles suggest.

3. **Huse (2005)** -- Behavioral governance and accountability framework. Supports studying informal governance mechanisms alongside formal ones.

4. **Aguilera and Jackson (2010)** -- Comparative governance survey. Provides the international comparison framework (German two-tier system) for contextualizing the US three-tier system.

5. **Gioia, Corley, and Hamilton (2013)** -- Gioia methodology for qualitative research. Provides the methodological framework that management journal referees will expect.

6. **Eisenhardt (1989)** -- Theory-building from case studies. Justifies the three-interview design for exploratory research.

7. **Davis, Schoorman, and Donaldson (1997)** -- Stewardship theory. Already mentioned in the research spec but should be more prominent -- Toj's reputation mechanism is a stewardship theory prediction.
